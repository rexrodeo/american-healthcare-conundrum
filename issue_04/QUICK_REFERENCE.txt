ISSUE #4 QUICK REFERENCE — "The Middlemen: Pharmacy Benefit Managers"
=======================================================================
Compiled: March 5, 2026 | Booked savings: $30B/year | Running total: $128.6B

THE ONE-LINE PITCH
------------------
Three companies process 80% of US prescriptions and extract ~$30B/year
through spread pricing, retained rebates, and formulary manipulation — all
invisible to the patients paying the bills.

THE SIX MECHANISMS OF EXTRACTION
----------------------------------
1. SPREAD PRICING
   PBMs bill health plans MORE than they reimburse pharmacies.
   Ohio: billed $224.8M more than they paid pharmacies in one year.
   Medicaid-scale estimate: $5B/year gross; book at $3B.

2. REBATE OPACITY
   Manufacturers pay $334B/year in rebates to secure favorable formulary placement.
   PBMs retain 5–10% above disclosed admin fees.
   Conservative: $10B/year retained by PBMs, not passed to plans or patients.

3. SPECIALTY DRUG MARKUP
   PBM-owned specialty pharmacies reimbursed at higher rates than independents.
   FTC documented: $7.3B over 5 years (2017–2022) = $1.5B/year ongoing.

4. FORMULARY MANIPULATION
   57.4% of ESI exclusions had questionable patient benefit.
   Brand insulins stay preferred; biosimilar insulins excluded — because brands pay
   bigger rebates. FDA-approved biosimilars: 100+. Penetration: far below Europe.
   Conservative savings from fixing this: $10B/year.

5. SELF-PREFERENCING
   PBMs steer patients to their own pharmacies via restricted networks.
   Florida: PBM-owned pharmacies = 0.4% of claims, 28% of profit.
   Embedded in specialty markup figures; not separately booked to avoid double-count.

6. INDEPENDENT PHARMACY DESTRUCTION
   Consequence of mechanisms above: rural pharmacy deserts.
   16.1% of rural independents closed 2003–2018.
   Some pharmacies: 80% of reimbursements below cost to dispense.
   This is a harm metric, not separately booked.

THE INSULIN CASE STUDY
-----------------------
Humalog price: $21 (1996) → $316 (2022) = +1,405%
Mechanism: Three manufacturers competed on rebate size (not price) to win PBM formulary
  placement. High rebates required high list prices. PBMs preferred high-list/high-rebate
  brands over cheaper biosimilar insulins (Semglee at $98, Rezvoglar at $92).
IRA cap: $35/month for Medicare beneficiaries (2023) — helps at the register but
  doesn't change the underlying rebate incentive structure.
FTC: Sued all 3 Big PBMs in September 2024 specifically over insulin pricing practices.
Biosimilar uptake: 20.6% market share in least-restrictive states (Kwon et al. 2025)
  — even with an FDA-approved interchangeable biosimilar, formulary barriers limit adoption.

THE VERTICAL INTEGRATION PROBLEM
----------------------------------
CVS Health = CVS Caremark PBM + CVS Pharmacy + Aetna insurer + specialty pharmacies
Cigna/Evernorth = Express Scripts PBM + Accredo specialty pharmacy
UnitedHealth = OptumRx PBM + OptumRx Mail Service + UHC insurer
Result: The entity that sets formularies and reimburses pharmacies ALSO owns the
  pharmacies being reimbursed at the highest rates.

POLICY STATUS
--------------
Already enacted:
  - IRA 2022: Part D rebate pass-through (fully effective 2025)
  - IRA 2022: $35 Medicare patient cap on insulin
  - Rutledge v. PCMA (2020): States can require PBMs to reimburse above cost
  - 15+ states have banned spread pricing in Medicaid (post-Ohio audit)
  - 40+ states have PBM reimbursement reform laws
What's left:
  - Federal spread pricing ban for ALL PBM programs (not just Medicaid)
  - Fiduciary standard for PBMs
  - Formulary transparency + biosimilar preference mandate
  - Vertical divestiture OR open network mandate
  - Federal right of pharmacies to refuse below-cost reimbursement
