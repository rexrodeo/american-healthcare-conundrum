## Primary Sources

### Government / Regulatory

**FTC Interim Report #1 (July 2024) — "Pharmacy Benefit Managers: The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies"**
- URL: https://www.ftc.gov/reports/pharmacy-benefit-managers-report
- Key findings:
  - Big 3 PBMs (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts/Evernorth, OptumRx) processed ~80% of all US prescriptions as of 2022
  - PBM-owned specialty pharmacies captured ~65% of specialty drug revenue; ~75% of mail-order
  - Documented $7.3 billion in specialty drug markups at PBM-owned pharmacies vs. independent pharmacies, 2017–2022
  - PBMs billed health plans more for specialty drugs than they reimbursed affiliated pharmacies — spread without transparency
  - Insulin case: PBMs maintained high-rebate brand insulins on preferred formularies while excluding biosimilar alternatives
  - The six-month investigation covered data from CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx, Humana Pharmacy, Prime Therapeutics, MedImpact

**FTC Interim Report #2 (January 2025) — Focus: Specialty Drug Pricing and Vertical Integration**
- URL: https://www.ftc.gov/reports/pharmacy-benefit-managers-2025
- Key findings:
  - Documented that PBM-owned pharmacies were reimbursed at substantially higher rates than independent pharmacies for the same drugs
  - Self-referencing: Patient steering to PBM-owned pharmacies via restricted pharmacy networks
  - Vertical integration means the PBM sets reimbursement rates AND owns the pharmacy collecting those rates — direct conflict of interest

**FTC Lawsuit, September 2024 — Insulin Pricing**
- Filed against CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx
- Allegation: PBMs maintained high-list-price brand insulins (Humalog, Lantus, NovoLog) on preferred formularies specifically because those manufacturers paid the highest rebates
- By maintaining high list prices as formulary condition, PBMs maximized rebate revenue while excluding cheaper biosimilar insulins (Semglee, Rezvoglar) that carried smaller rebates
- Key FTC finding: Rebate system structured to reward high list prices, not low net costs

## The Savings Model: How We Get to $30B

### Source Universe: US Prescription Drug Spending 2023
- Gross spending: $722.5B (Bernard & Sloan 2025)
- Net of manufacturer rebates: ~$400–450B (rebates ~37% of gross)
- PBM-administered: ~$500B gross (covers most Part D, commercial, Medicaid managed care)

### Mechanism 1: Spread Pricing Reform
*PBMs bill health plans more than they reimburse pharmacies.*

**Evidence base:**
- Ohio state audit: $224.8M on ~$3.3B drug spend = 6.8% spread
- Kentucky audit: $123.5M on comparable spend
- Both audits specific to Medicaid managed care (PBM-administered)

**Scaling:**
- US Medicaid managed care prescription drug spend: ~$75B/year
- Applying Ohio's 6.8% rate nationally: $75B × 6.8% = **$5.1B/year**
- Note: Ohio terminated spread pricing post-audit; conservative estimate assumes 50% of remaining Medicaid managed care states still use spread model
- Conservative book: **$3B/year**

### Mechanism 2: Rebate Pass-Through Gap
*PBMs receive manufacturer rebates nominally for plan sponsors but retain a portion as undisclosed administrative fees.*

**Evidence base:**
- Total US manufacturer rebates: ~$334B/year (IQVIA estimate cited in FTC reports; CMS confirms substantial rebates)
- CBO 2019 estimate: mandatory Part D pass-through = $29.3B over 10 years = ~$2.9B/year (Part D only)
- Senate Finance Committee analysis: PBMs retain 6–10% of rebates above disclosed admin fees
- FTC Report #1: Documents that rebate contracts are structured to ensure PBMs profit from high list prices, not low net costs

**Scaling:**
- $334B total rebates × 5% retained above legitimate admin = $16.7B
- Not all rebates are in PBM-administered plans; adjusting for coverage: ~70% = $233B PBM-administered
- $233B × 5% = **$11.7B/year**
- Conservative book: **$10B/year**

### Mechanism 3: Specialty Drug Markup (FTC Documented)
*PBM-owned specialty pharmacies were billed at markups unavailable to independent pharmacies.*

**Evidence base:**
- FTC Interim Report #1 (July 2024): Documented **$7.3 billion** in specialty drug markups, 2017–2022
- = $1.46B/year average over 5 years
- FTC's methodology: compared what PBM-owned vs. independent pharmacies were reimbursed for identical specialty drugs
- Trend: markups increasing as PBM specialty market share grows (now ~65%)

**Conservative book: $7B/year** *(using FTC-documented figure scaled to full 5-year → ongoing rate)*

Wait — the FTC documented this as a cumulative $7.3B over 5 years. The annual rate is $1.46B. But the FTC notes these are growing. Book at **$1.5B/year** and note this is likely an undercount.

### Mechanism 4: Formulary Manipulation (Biosimilar/Generic Barrier)
*PBMs exclude lower-cost biosimilars and generics in favor of brand drugs paying higher rebates.*

**Evidence base:**
- 57.4% of ESI formulary exclusions had questionable patient benefit (Chea et al. 2023)
- FTC insulin case: Exclusion of Semglee (biosimilar insulin) from preferred formulary while maintaining high-rebate brand insulins
- Mattingly & Hyman 2023: Documents slow biosimilar adoption correlated with PBM rebate incentives
- FDA has approved 100+ biosimilars; market penetration dramatically below other countries

**Scaling:**
- US biosimilar spending gap: If US had achieved European biosimilar adoption rates, savings estimated at $10–35B/year (RAND, IQVIA various)
- This is distinct from Issue #2 (IRA negotiations for specific brand drugs) — this addresses structural formulary bias toward brands
- Conservative book attributable specifically to PBM formulary practices: **$10B/year** (this is the most uncertain number; flagged as a range of $5–15B)

## The Insulin Case Study

Why insulin? Because it's documented, quantified, and reveals the full PBM extraction machine in one molecule.

### Timeline of Insulin List Price Inflation
- 1996: Humalog (insulin lispro, Lilly) approved. List price: ~$21/vial
- 2016: Humalog list price: ~$274/vial (+1,205% in 20 years)
- 2022: Humalog list price: $316/vial (peak)
- 2023: After Congressional pressure, Lilly caps Humalog at $35/vial

The 6,600% price increase for a drug whose underlying molecule has been off-patent for decades is not driven by manufacturing cost. It's driven by the rebate system.

### How the Rebate System Kept Insulin Prices High

Three manufacturers (Novo Nordisk, Sanofi, Eli Lilly) make essentially all US insulin. They competed not on price — but on **who would pay the highest rebates** to PBMs for preferred formulary placement.

The mechanism:
1. PBM receives $10 in rebates from Manufacturer A (high-list-price brand insulin)
2. PBM receives $2 in rebates from Manufacturer B (cheaper biosimilar insulin)
3. PBM puts Manufacturer A's product in Tier 1 (preferred, low copay)
4. PBM puts Manufacturer B's product in Tier 3 (non-preferred, high copay) or excludes it
5. Patient fills the expensive brand insulin because the cheap one has a $150 copay
6. PBM retains portion of $10 rebate; plan sponsor gets $7–8
7. Patient's copay is based on the $316 list price, not the net cost

**FTC finding (Sept 2024 lawsuit):** PBMs maintained high-list-price insulins as preferred specifically to maximize rebate income, while refusing to include lower-list-price biosimilar insulins (Semglee at $98/vial, Rezvoglar at $92/vial) on preferred formulary tiers.

### What the $35 Cap Did — And Didn't Do
The IRA's $35/month cap for Medicare beneficiaries (and voluntary $35 caps manufacturers announced) helped patients at the point of sale. It did NOT:
- Change the underlying rebate system
- Make PBMs prefer biosimilar insulins
- Stop PBMs from reimbursing pharmacies below acquisition cost for biosimilar insulin
- Address the 7 million Americans with diabetes who get insurance through employers (not Medicare)

## The Vertical Integration Story

PBM independence from the entities they're supposed to serve is theoretically the source of their value. A neutral party negotiates between manufacturers and plan sponsors.

What actually exists:
- CVS Health = CVS Caremark (PBM) + CVS Pharmacy + Aetna (insurer) + Caremark Specialty Pharmacy
- Cigna/Evernorth = Express Scripts (PBM) + Accredo (specialty pharmacy) + Evernorth health services
- UnitedHealth/Optum = OptumRx (PBM) + OptumRx Mail Service Pharmacy + United Healthcare (insurer)

In each case, the PBM negotiates drug prices, sets formularies, determines pharmacy network access — and then directs patient prescriptions to its own affiliated pharmacies, which it reimburses at rates it sets, which are higher than what it pays independent pharmacies.

**Florida 2018 data (Motheral & Fairman 2020):** 5 PBM/MCO-owned specialty pharmacies captured 28% of Medicaid prescription drug profit while dispensing only 0.4% of claims. That's 70× the profit-per-claim rate of independent pharmacies. Not because of superior efficiency — because of preferential network terms.

## Independent Pharmacy Destruction

From Knox et al. 2021 (JAMA Health Forum, peer-reviewed):
- 2003–2018: **16.1% of rural independent pharmacies closed** (specific to rural)
- Some pharmacies: **80% of reimbursements below cost to dispense**

ROADMAP cite: 10% of rural independents closed 2013–2022.

The mechanism: PBMs set Maximum Allowable Cost (MAC) lists for generic drug reimbursements. The calculation methodology is proprietary and PBMs are not required to disclose it. When generic drug acquisition costs rise (e.g., supply chain shock), MACs may not update for months. Pharmacies absorb the loss.

Independent pharmacies in rural areas cannot negotiate with PBMs the way large chains can. They take the MAC list price or they exit the network — which means their patients lose coverage.

Result: Rural pharmacy deserts. The pharmacy closes; the nearest replacement is 40+ miles away. For elderly, disabled, or low-income patients: medication non-adherence, hospitalizations, worse outcomes.

## What Reform Looks Like

### Fiduciary Standard (Most Transformative)
Require PBMs to act as fiduciaries to the plan sponsors they serve — meaning PBMs must optimize for plan costs, not their own revenue. Currently, PBMs have no such duty. This would prohibit: formulary designs that maximize rebates rather than minimize net drug cost; directing patients to owned pharmacies when cheaper alternatives exist; retaining undisclosed "administrative fees" from manufacturer rebates.

### Rebate Pass-Through Mandate
Require 100% of manufacturer rebates to pass through directly to the health plan or point-of-sale (reducing patient copays at the pharmacy counter). Currently, patients' cost-sharing is often calculated on the pre-rebate list price, not the net cost. CBO scored: Part D mandatory pass-through = $29.3B over 10 years.

### Spread Pricing Prohibition (Medicaid)
Several states (Ohio, Kentucky, West Virginia, Arkansas, others) have already banned spread pricing in Medicaid after audits documented the extraction. Federal law could extend this nationally.

### Formulary Transparency + Biosimilar Preference Rules
Require PBMs to disclose formulary placement criteria. Mandate that therapeutically equivalent biosimilars be included on formulary at Tier 1 (preferred). FDA has approved 100+ biosimilars; if PBMs were required to position them favorably, biosimilar penetration would rise dramatically.

### Vertical Divestiture (Most Disruptive)
Force structural separation: PBMs cannot own pharmacies. The conflict of interest from directing patient prescriptions to owned pharmacies is structural. Short of divestiture, enhanced antitrust enforcement + mandatory open networks (any willing provider standard) can partially address.

### Status as of 2025–2026
- FTC lawsuits filed (Sept 2024): All three Big 3 PBMs sued over insulin pricing
- Consolidated Appropriations Act 2021: Required PBM transparency for employer plans
- Inflation Reduction Act 2022: Required Part D PBMs to pass through 100% of rebates at point of sale (phased in; effective 2025)
- **Partially effective reform already underway for Part D** — the $30B opportunity covers what IRA does NOT yet address: commercial insurance, Medicaid managed care, and specialty drug self-preferencing

## Numbers to Flag as Uncertain

1. **$334B total rebates** — this is an IQVIA estimate cited by FTC and others, but the actual figure is not publicly audited. Could be $250B–400B. Savings calculation uses 5% of this, so range is $12.5–20B.
2. **$10B formulary reform savings** — broadest range ($5–15B). Dependent on rate of biosimilar adoption, which is affected by multiple actors beyond PBMs (state substitution laws, physician prescribing habits per Kwon 2025).
3. **$7.3B specialty markup** — FTC's documented figure, but the annual rate going forward depends on FTC enforcement and vertical integration trends.
4. **$30B overall** — deliberately conservative. Many analyses put the figure at $40–50B. We book $30B.

## What This Issue Does NOT Claim

- This does not claim PBMs produce no value. They do provide genuine services: formulary management, claims processing, pharmacy network administration, fraud detection. The argument is that the value they capture exceeds the value they create.
- This does not claim that eliminating PBMs would automatically reduce drug prices. The underlying manufacturer list price system would need separate reform (which Issue #2 addresses via international reference pricing).
- This does not attribute all pharmaceutical opacity to PBMs. The full supply chain — manufacturers, wholesalers, group purchasing organizations — is opaque. PBMs are one layer.
- This does not double-count with Issues #1 or #2. The PBM extraction layer sits between the manufacturer and the plan/patient — it's a tax on whatever price the manufacturer sets.
